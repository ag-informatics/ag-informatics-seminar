The Origin of the Luddite

[A Person] opposed or resistant to new technologies or technological change.

Content Notes: - Our resistance to technology is also historical. While we use the term Luddite to describe a person opposed to technology, this phrase came from a movement involving "frame-breakers", people opposed to mechanization in textiles and oppression through technology. This revolutionary act of technological destruction was eventually punishable with the death penalty by 1812. References: -

Revolutions with Consequences

What does the learner learn? - Content Notes: - The tools we build have the power to transform the earth and our society. The Technological Revolution of the late 19th to early 20th century was marked by exponential economic growth. The development of iron and steel-making processes let us design complex engines engines, larger-than-life buildings, bridges, and transportation systems, with dramatic social and environmental impacts. Techne and Politeia

Technology as...
"the interweaving of useful devices, technical systems, philosophical ideals, institutional arrangements, and civic practices that together express the quality of life in any political society"
Content Notes: But there are far-reaching, long-term, social and political impacts because of how we design and even build technological infrastructure. South Asian indentured laborers were brought to East Africa by British colonialists to construct the very railroads that they used as they fled Uganda during the Idi Amin regime. Consider then, that technology is "the interweaving of useful devices, technical systems, philosophical ideals, institutional arrangements, and civic practices that together express the quality of life in any political society" References: -

Design in the Anthropocene

"[O]ur species, Homo sapiens, has had such a significant impact on Earth and its inhabitants that we will have a lasting - and potentially irreversible - influence on its systems, environment, processes and biodiversity.

The Earth is 4.5 billion years old, and modern humans have been around for around a mere 200,000 years. Yet in that time we have fundamentally altered the physical, chemical and biological systems of the planet on which we and all other organisms depend.

In the past 60 years in particular, these human impacts have unfolded at an unprecedented rate and scale. This period is sometimes known as the Great Acceleration.
Content Notes: - Though the Anthropocene isn't necessarily a settled geological epoch, it describes as a time period defined by significant human impact on the world around us. This significant human impact involved humans reshaping the earth through the creation of large scale infrastructure and a techno-centric mindset. The evolution of technology is, in fact, multicultural, multifaceted, and multitudinous. We build on each others ideas and knowledge, sometimes taking without consent, other times learning how to work together to create mutually supportive technologies. References: -

The Great Acceleration

Content Notes: - The marriage of advances in science and engineering led to electrification and fueled our ongoing relationship with coal. We networked our environment through railroads, sewage systems, and even the telegraph, connecting and amplifying our impacts on the world. References: -

Technological Change

Content Notes: - If we zoom out from where we are today, we can see the the bidirectional influence of technology on society and vice versa. I trust that we can agree that technology is old, and we have had centuries to refine our design processes. This notion of "design' is also not new, but our approach, our guiding philosophies, values, principles, and requirements have evolved. We can, if we choose to, reflect on how to grapple with the complexity of the design challenges that lies ahead. References: -

The Promised Digital-Future-Land

Content Notes: - Today, in the mid-21st century, we have more information about our past that ever before, the opportunity to dynamically interact with and influence our current path, and are equipped with tools to speculate and hypothesize intelligently about futures to come. The ongoing information revolution presents another curious moment in human history, where we are increasingly producing technology to help us manage complexity at scale. References: -

Design on Spaceship Earth in the Long Now

Content Notes: - The design of technology does not happen in a bubble of engineers and scientists. These scientific and technological revolutions, happen through a blend of collaborative and competitive human action, our inaction, and healthy dose of serendipity, or luck. Wars and famines, ecological disasters and space races, the changing social, political, and ecological landscapes drive funding and interest in the design of entire genres of technology. -The ongoing Anthropocene is dramatic period in human time on earth, as we barrel forward, changing our environment, our ways of working, and even ourselves, through the application of the science to through technology. We continue to apply science through engineering as we design streamlined processes, complex systems, and radical technologies. In this course, I invite you to consider, what it means to pro-actively adopt a human-centered and ecologically-oriented approach to DESIGNing technology beyond the Anthropocene. References: -

Design for the Long Now

What does the learner learn? - Content Notes: - With the catalytic progress in digital technology, including contemporary developments in the design of artificial intelligence, I think it prudent to strive for deliberacy, thoughtfulness, and long-term systems thinking as we continue to shape our journey on Earth and beyond. - We're at a strange point in time right now where technology feels both frightening and exciting. We feel closer to that science-fiction future that we dreamt about, but are confronted by complex realities, including climate change, pandemics, war and poverty. In this course, I confront that conflict. I embrace the tension to help me think critically about how I design technologies. I invite you to do the same. References: -
